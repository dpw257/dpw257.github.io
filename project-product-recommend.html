Customer Segmentation and Product Recommend Engine with Dynamic Pricing

An electronics company decided to target existing customers with suggested products and discounts tailored to their purchasing behaviour and overall value to the company, in order to maximise customer loyalty and increase revenue.

Customers were segmented based on the value and recency of purchases, and a set recommended products were found for each customer. Prices of the entire product range were updated dynamically according to recent sales trends, quantity in stock and competitor benchmarking, before adding personalised discounts according to each customer's value segment.

1. Project objectives

  ●   Segment customers according to recency, frequency and monetary value of their purchases (RFM scores).
  ●   Cluster products using TF-IDF of product descriptions and recommend product for each customer.
  ●   Scrape and clean competitor prices from price comparison site.
  ●   Assess success rate with different demographics and suggest improvements.
  ●   Set prices for recommended products based on competitor pricing, cost, sales history and quantity in stock and apply personalised discounts.


2. Methodology

2.1. Data collection and preprocessing


The customer dataset contains basic demographics data, a record of recently purchased products and the recency, frequency and monetary value of purchases (RFM).

The product dataset was scraped from a price comparison site using BeautifulSoup. Its data includes product descriptions and the current sale prices of competitors.

During the cleaning process, duplicate products, products with descriptions in languages other than English, and products lacking competitor price data were removed. Each customer was randomly assigned products for their purchase history.


2.2. Customer segmentation


Customers were segmented using RFM scores, based on the frequency (total number) of purchases, the recency of the last purchase in the past 36 months, and the total monetary of value to date. Each customer's values for recency, frequency, and monetary value were converted to single digits from 1 to 3, and these three digits were concatenated to give a three-digit RFM score denoting the customers overall value. There were 27 possible RFM scores in total, from the lowest score of 111, to the highest, 333.


Figure 1: Recency, Frequency, Monetary values converted to RFM scores.


The 27 RFM scores were divided into five groups to create five customer segments based on customer value to the company, allowing separate discounts to be defined for each segment.


Figure 2: Customers segmented on value (RFM scores)


2.3. Product clustering and recommendation system


The similarity of products was determined by analysing the text of each product's description. Each text was tokenised, stopwords were removed and a TF-IDF score (term frequency-inverse document frequency) was calculated and stored as a vector.

The cosine distance between each vectors was calculated (figure 3) to determine the most similar products to each customers most recently purchased product. Several products were selected for each customer to be sold individually and as part of a bundle.


Figure 3: Products clustered by TD-IDF of descriptions


Figure 4: Products recommended for each previous purchase


2.4. Dynamic pricing


The prices of the entire product list was updated dynamically based on current stock levels and recent upward or downward trends in sales to encourage sales of slow-moving products and maximise revenue from popular products. To ensure all prices remain below the most expensive competitor, a further reduction was applied to products exceeding this benchmark.

Personalised discounts were applied for each customer segment to reward existing customers and encourage lost high-value customers to return. A check was run to ensure all prices remain above a minimum profit margin of 10%.


Figure 5: Dynamically defined new standard prices and personalised discount prices


2.5. Limitations


Stopwords and other common words were removed from product descriptions, since they could influence the TF-IDF values. For this task, removing this context does not create significant ambiguity. In addition, removing stopwords reduces the number of features, which contributes to a reduction in computational cost.


3. Results

Figure 6: A product selected for a customer with new calculated price


The dynamic pricing process resulted in a price increase for 12% of products and a decrease for 17% of products. 8% of products were then found to exceed the highest competitor benchmark, so a further reduction was applied.

After applying the personalised discounts, 10% of product prices fell below the minimum profit margin, so these prices were adjusted to meet the minimum rate.

Customer buying behaviour varies significantly by customer type (Business, Student, Standard). Customers in the business category purchased fewer bundles than other categories and bought less recommended products overall. Conversely, students buy the most bundles of any customer type. Female customers in the Standard category rank second in bundle purchases and show equal interest in bundles and individual products, buying the most recommended products of any customer type. Standard male customers purchase fewer bundles than women but buy the most individual items.


Figure 7: Response rate of different demographics


4. Insights

The results highlight the importance of tailoring marketing approaches to each customer segment. Continual improvement can be achieved through ongoing monitoring of the recommendation system's success rates, enabling informed adjustments to the marketing strategy.

In the future, A/B testing could be conducted on price to determine the price sensitivity of distinct customer types, allowing for more refined pricing strategies to be implemented.
